The Great Unknown Artist of Chauvet Cave

< Back Home

30,000 years before the advent of mass media, when we still lurked in the bowels of the earth, an artist painted this bear upon a cavern wall. Notice how it has no practical use. It did not feed the tribe. It did not protect their bivouac. It simply exists to exist. It is just an image, slightly more permanent than the artist’s own shadow cast by a flickering flame.

They must have drawn it by memory, of course. Bears do not willingly sit to have their portraits taken. This was long before the possibility of domestication. Humanity was still very much just another rung on the food chain. We were hunted as much as the hunter. Every memory of the bear was fear and death. Perhaps this cave was once the bear’s lair, and its depths were littered with the bones of the artist’s brethren. But now, it is cold stone. Nothing more.

You see, the bear was an existential terror to early humans. The works of Lovecraft and Barron are merely cultural memories of the eldritch horror we faced in Eden. The bear was fang and claw, and we were soft meat for chewing. Before we sharpened sticks and stones to break their bones, we hunted by throwing our bodies on our prey in hopes that we may smother them to death with our sheer numbers. Often, we were thrown around like a drunk girl on the mechanical bull. Few survived a broken bone in those days. But this was life. This is how we lived. Nowadays people think 100 men couldn’t beat a single gorilla. In the time of the antediluvians, leopards leaped out of trees. Crocodiles lurked in the rivers. We hadn’t even learned what mushrooms were tasty snacks, psychedelic trips, or instant death. Nowhere on earth was safe for us hairless apes. We are not talking about a starving artist in a metaphorical sense. Hunger was the natural state of man. One good hunt was the difference between the tribe surviving another day and the extinction of the entire human race. So we soldiered on.

And yet, despite this bleak existence, some cave dwelling Paleolithic humanoid, maybe not even a homo sapien for all we know, crushed up some berries that could have eased their hunger pains for a while at least. They stood in near darkness, with the same eyes we have today, straining to see as far as their arm could reach, and they immortalized this bear for all the world to see.

Why? Why do it at all?

Look at it! What do you see?

The anatomy is flawless, true. Especially considering again, it is drawn from memory.

The lines, skillful and precise. Imagine mastering this craft while predators hunt you for sustenance, blessed sustenance.

But it more than that. The bear is cute.

They made the bear cute. A savage beast, death incarnate, and the artist made it cute. Look at its soft, round ears. How its snout sniffs at the air while its mouth curves into a sly smile. If you told me this was concept art for some children’s cartoon I would believe you. But Winnie the Pooh, this is not.

Why depict such a terrifying creature in a harmless, almost whimsical, fashion?

Maybe that was the point.

Bears are scary and to live in this world we must fight them. They eat our friends. But we are soft and squishy, blood bags for the carnage. Everyone knows what a bear looks like these days, and still people see a bear and mistake it for the Jersey Devil. So how do you prepare the tribe for their war with a terrifying enemy? You make a graven image of the devil to strip it of its power. The image cannot harm you. You leave it as a message to all that come after you.

“I was here once. I saw a bear. Bears are scary, but I survived, so can you.”

In mordern times, art has become a profession. People make art to sell, as a way of escaping poverty. But art is not a product for consumption. Art is life. Art is communication. Art is something we have done since before the invention of language. Van Gogh could talk your ear off about his sadness, but Stary Night captures the great melancholy and the beauty he saw in the world despite it more than words ever could. That is the point of art. There are far more realistic drawings of bears made by better artists. Hell, even soulless autonomations can make more *accurate* pictures of bears. But this ink scratched into stone by some proto human with just a little free time between maulings and starvation will always mean more to me than all the creations of the Artists^tm and AI because it is the absolute word of God themself. It is the inscription left on the gates of Eden.

It is a metaphor for existence.

The bear seemed an impossible threat to our ancestors. The same can be said for the bears of our generation. One day, we will not have to fight the bear anymore. But until then, keep your spearheads sharp and maybe leave a warning to those who come after you. Life is suffering, but it is more pleasant with some paint on the walls.

Stay safe, do not fear the bear. I survived, so can you.
